## Argument Structure

The "AI wrote your paper" objection is the first thing a skeptical reader brings to CT. Answering it up front reframes what CT actually is before any positive claims are made.

### 1. The critique and its two versions

Colloquial: AI wrote your paper. Academic: cognitive offloading (HCI literature). Both conflate retrieval with generation, and pipeline speed with authorship.

### 2. The Extended Mind misreading

Clark and Chalmers' Extended Mind thesis is about the *boundary* of cognition, not the delegation of it. The offloading framing already misreads the thesis — when a notebook extends memory, cognition does not leave the agent, the agent's boundary expands. "Cognitive offloading" applied to AI-assisted intellectual work misdescribes the phenomenon twice: it misappropriates the theoretical source and misdescribes what is actually happening.

### 3. Workload outsourcing — the precise alternative

*[Expand from workload-outsourcing.md — the labor moves, the judgment stays; the question does the work; retrieval serves a prepared conceptual space or it doesn't land]*

### 4. The Chowdhury contrast

Moral outsourcing is dangerous because judgment transfers. Workload outsourcing is legitimate because judgment doesn't. The parallel structure makes the normative distinction crisp and positions CT clearly: CT is a workload outsourcing architecture that structurally prevents judgment outsourcing by keeping the human as conductor.

### 6. RIP and RIFF as the operational formalization

CT does not just make this argument theoretically — it operationalizes the distinction. RIP is the workload outsourcing phase: surface area expansion, connection retrieval, low epistemic friction. RIFF is where authorship happens: structured friction, judgment engaged, synthesis owned. The two-mode architecture embeds the workload/judgment distinction in the interaction design itself.
