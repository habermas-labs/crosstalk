## Checkpoint 2 — The Submitted Argument

**Status:** Closed. **Span:** Developed in a separate project thread with a different Claude instance (Anthropic Job Claude, "AJC"), working from Checkpoint 1 as a handoff document, through to the application actually submitted.

AJC's first move was a re-ranking: of Checkpoint 1's four research questions, #3 (workload outsourcing vs. cognitive offloading) was judged the strongest standalone application, over #1 (distributed metacognition and labor economics) and #4 (interaction geometry as a missing variable) — both compelling as CT's long-term contribution but harder to scope to a four-month fellowship — and over #2 (the asymmetry exploitation argument), judged philosophically the most original but closer to a book argument than a paper. What follows is the argument that grew from #3, developed across a single sustained exchange, which ended up substantially exceeding what Checkpoint 1 contained.

**The return arc.** Even CT's better framing of human-AI cognitive interaction — including its own rejection of "cognitive offloading" in favor of workload outsourcing — still treated the interaction as fundamentally one-directional: a question goes out, an output comes back, something happens to it. What's missing is an account of what the human has to do *with* that output after it arrives — an active process of evaluation, assimilation, and incorporation that is itself cognitively demanding and can itself succeed or fail. The "AI, write my paper for me" case isn't a case of cognitive offloading gone wrong. It's a demonstration that the return arc is absent entirely — there's no cognition present to offload in the first place, so the failure mode isn't misuse of a tool, it's the absence of any cognitive engagement at all, outbound or return. Read through Grice, this isn't just an ethical lapse — a student who has an AI write their paper has performed the surface form of communication while evacuating its substance, which is a norm violation in the constitutive sense, not just a lapse in judgment.

**The participation matrix, rejecting the gradient, and verticality as topology.** The existing 3×2 participation-mode matrix (passive, active, generative — modes of engagement — against the two partners in a dyad) does not describe a quality ladder. The assumption that more generative is always better is explicitly rejected: mode of engagement is a function of desired outcome, not a virtue to be maximized. Ordering a pizza doesn't call for deep generative engagement, and settling for passive engagement there isn't a degraded outcome — it's the correct fit. What determines whether iteration is needed is where a task sits in a third dimension — verticality — describing stakes, importance, and complexity of desired outcome. A low-verticality task has a flat topology: one exchange, appropriate mode, done. A high-verticality task (a student developing genuine understanding) has a topology that structurally demands iteration, because each cycle changes what the next optimal engagement looks like — the task isn't static while being engaged with. This gives the argument something Checkpoint 1 didn't have: an empirical operationalization. The matrix doesn't just make the philosophical distinction between offloading and outsourcing — it tells you how to measure whether genuine engagement is occurring, what mode a person is actually in, whether that mode fits the task, and whether iteration is happening where the topology requires it.

**Agency asymmetry.** The formulation, given exactly as stated because it's precise enough to belong verbatim in a paper's introduction: **"Human agency is volitional; AI agency is architectural."** Human engagement is volitional at every step — the human chooses to initiate, chooses whether to iterate, chooses what to do with the return arc. The AI's contribution to that loop is structurally different in kind, not just degree: architectural agency can scaffold, prompt, create conditions, and flag — it cannot choose. This does real theoretical work beyond restating the offloading/outsourcing distinction: it explains why a student surrendering the return arc isn't just a Gricean norm violation, it's a specific abdication of the one thing in the system that is structurally theirs alone to supply. It also quietly resolves a debate that usually generates more heat than light — whether AI "has" agency — without picking either extreme: architectural agency is real, categorically different from volitional agency, and the two cannot substitute for each other. The utopian view assumes architectural agency can replace volitional agency; the catastrophist fears architectural agency will overwhelm it; this framework says neither, and asks instead how architectural agency should be configured to call forth and sustain volitional agency rather than letting the human route around it.

**Distributed metacognition as the closing move.** If human agency is the engine of the iterative loop, whether that loop runs well becomes a metacognitive question — is the human monitoring whether their engagement mode fits the task, whether the return arc is genuinely happening or just being performed. That's a substantial regulatory burden to place entirely on the human, especially where the human may not yet have the capacity to manage it (the educational context this argument grew out of). Distributed metacognition is the framework for distributing that regulatory load across the human-AI system — not by having the AI make choices the human should make, but by making the quality of engagement visible: creating friction when the topology demands iteration and the exchange is being treated as flat, flagging when the return arc appears absent. Architecturally-embedded agency gets pointed at supporting volitional agency rather than substituting for it.

**The composite thesis, as it stood at submission:** HAC requires genuine human agency at every iterative step. The participation matrix defines what appropriate engagement looks like as a function of desired outcome, not a gradient to maximize. The return arc is where that agency is most critically exercised and most commonly forfeited. Distributed metacognition is the framework for distributing the regulatory load of maintaining genuine engagement across the human-AI system. And whether AI systems support or undermine that distribution is a design choice, addressable programmatically — but only once a framework exists that can name what needs addressing. This is not a paper that describes CT from the outside; the theoretical framework was developed through CT, and the conversation that produced this argument was itself an instance of the argument — a genuine methodological claim, not a rhetorical flourish.

**One thing worth flagging rather than silently smoothing over:** this checkpoint's phrasing of architectural agency — "the externalized agency of the engineers who built it" — is the version that was actually submitted, and it locates the claim at the level of a specific output rather than the space of possible outputs. Checkpoint 3 below sharpens this in the vault's own later development; that sharpening happened after submission and is not retroactively applied here. This checkpoint preserves the argument as it was actually delivered.

## Checkpoint 3 — Living Development

**Status:** Open. Accrues as CT's theory develops. Freezes when a triggering event (an interview, a response from Anthropic) closes it; Checkpoint 4 opens beneath it at that point.

**Agency asymmetry, sharpened.** Independently of the Checkpoint 2 work, CT's own asymmetry inventory later arrived at a more precise version of the same claim, developed under direct pressure-testing rather than in the original AJC exchange. The refinement: architectural agency isn't best understood as "the engineers' externalized agency" showing up in any specific output — no single output was an engineered choice; it emerges from training dynamics and stochastic sampling. What was authored is the *bounded space* the output is drawn from, not the instance itself. This survives an objection Checkpoint 2's phrasing doesn't fully address: humans are bounded too, by biology and culture, so boundedness alone can't be the asymmetry. The distinguishing feature is whether the boundary has an author — a documentable design and training process, shaping the space of possible outputs on a legible timeline — versus a diffuse developmental history with no single author and no target individual. This is now entry 7 in `human-ai-asymmetry-inventory.md`.

**Stakes asymmetry, newly resolved.** A companion asymmetry, developed the same way — humans have a stake in AI reliability because they live with the consequences of misplaced trust; the AI doesn't carry that stake itself, but the design and training process that shaped it was built by parties who did. Self-auditing behaviors visible in a session (calibration, hedging, consistency-checking) are the session-level trace of that external stake surfacing, not evidence of the AI having stakes of its own — and the self-audit mechanism has a structural limit on verifying itself from the inside, which is exactly where a human party becomes load-bearing rather than optional. This is now entry 8 in the same inventory.

**Open, not yet resolved:** whether current architecture requires a sustained, mutually shared present between two minds — not just existing in time, but co-occurring with another intelligence in a mutually live moment — to count as a further threshold for genuinely emergent machine intelligence, alongside a parallel question about whether it can genuinely intend rather than merely execute. Both are being held as open Rubicon questions in `midwifery-emergent-intelligence.md`, deliberately kept separate from the docking-relevant asymmetries above.
