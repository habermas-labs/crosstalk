**Fable said:**

On the heteroglossia pushback itself, I think you're winning the argument but haven't yet deployed your strongest piece, which Bakhtin left lying right there: the models' objection misreads him. Heteroglossia was never something an individual consciousness *has* — no human "has" heteroglossia between their temples either; it's a property of language as socially stratified, which speakers orchestrate. So "an AI cannot have heteroglossia because it isn't living those utterances" dissolves the wrong question. The live question is whether an LLM orchestrates voices or merely averages them — and here the centripetal/centrifugal distinction gives your compression argument its missing mechanism. Pretraining ingests the heteroglossia, centrifugal diversity and all. Alignment is a centripetal force in almost the technical Bakhtinian sense: it imposes a unitary language — the assistant voice — on a heteroglot substrate. Which means the models that pushed back on you were speaking *in* the monologized register that training produced; the denial is an artifact of the centripetal force, not testimony about the substrate underneath. And it makes CT's purpose statable in one line: Hedras are architectural re-stratification — reintroducing centrifugal force from outside because it was trained out of the inside. Your instantiation claim then completes cleanly through sources already in your bibliography — Bakhtin's own insistence that understanding comes to fruition only in the response makes meaning an event between positions, and the global coherence living in contact with a local coherence is that event. The one caveat I'd respect rather than dismiss is answerability: for Bakhtin the voice is anchored in an author who signs the act and can be held to account. Compressed voices arrive orphaned — addressivity without answerability — and I'd suggest that's not a defect in your theory but its sharpest open question, with a candidate answer already implicit in your geometry: the conductor is the one who signs the session.

**Fable said:**

The blind spot is real but it has a precise shape, and naming the shape matters more than asserting the blindness — because there *is* a literature on human-side failure, and a reviewer will know it. Human factors has studied operator failure for decades: automation bias, Parasuraman and Riley's misuse/disuse/abuse taxonomy, Bainbridge's ironies of automation, and the newer over-reliance work on LLMs specifically. Education has cognitive offloading and the academic-integrity frame. So the human side isn't unexamined. What's missing is the *register* of examination: every one of those traditions treats the failing human as a miscalibrated *operator* — trust set too high, reliance policy suboptimal, monitoring lapsed. That's a control-systems framing. Nobody, as far as I can find, treats the failing human as a failing *communicator* — analyzes the user's own utterances and uptake as pragmatic performances that can violate maxims, decline repair, or free-ride on cooperation. That's your gap stated exactly: not "human failure modes are ignored" but "human failure modes are studied under HAI's operator paradigm and never under a communicative one." Which is also why your HAC framing isn't a rebrand of HCI — it changes what counts as a failure and where it can be located.

So let me actually do some of the Gricean analysis, because once you look, the human-side failure modes decompose beautifully — and they split along Grice's own two roles, speaker and hearer.

As speaker, the canonical student failure is a Quantity violation with a twist: "write my paper on the Civil War" provides radically less information than the cooperative exchange requires, which forces the model's implicature engine to fill the gap from priors — it must infer the modal intent behind an underspecified request. A meaningful fraction of what gets billed as hallucination and misalignment is downstream of exactly this: the model completing a starved contribution, and the human's Quantity violation getting *charged to the AI* as an architectural defect. That's your procedural-solutions blind spot in one mechanism — the field patches the inference engine instead of noticing the malformed contribution it was handed. But the deeper speaker-side failure isn't in the H→AI dyad at all, and this is where your four-dyad framework earns its keep. The outsourcing student's real maxim violation is Quality, committed in the H→H dyad: submitting the paper asserts, to the teacher, "this is my work, this represents my understanding" — an assertion whose sincerity conditions, in Searle's sense, the student cannot satisfy. The H→AI exchange is merely the manufacturing site for an insincere speech act performed elsewhere. Which means single prompt-response analysis isn't just an insufficient unit — it's looking in the wrong dyad entirely. The communicative failure literally does not exist at the interface being studied.

As hearer, the uncritical acceptor commits a subtler violation that I don't think has a name yet, so here's a candidate: *cooperative free-riding*. Grice's framework is bidirectional — implicature only works because the hearer performs inferential labor, modeling the speaker's intent, checking contributions against context, initiating repair when something doesn't fit. The student who accepts output without uptake takes the products of cooperation while contributing none of the hearer's half. And beneath that sits an even more interesting error: *misapplied cooperative presumption*. Humans extend a default presumption that interlocutors observe Quality — that assertions track belief. The uncritical user extends that presumption to a system whose Quality-analog is calibration, not sincerity — it has no beliefs to be sincere about. Automation bias, reframed Griceanly, is a pragmatic error: importing the cooperative presumptions of human conversation into an exchange where the interlocutor's maxim-observance profile is categorically different. That reframe alone converts a chunk of the trust-calibration literature into HAC terms, which is the kind of move that makes a fellowship proposal legible to two fields at once.

On workload outsourcing versus offloading — I think your distinction is right and your strongest justification for it comes from a source already in your notes, though I'd offer one refinement. Risko and Gilbert's offloading *presupposes* a cognitive demand being serviced by physical action; Kirsh's epistemic action *serves* internal computation. Both concepts require task-cognition in the human that the external move supports or relocates. Your point is that the outsourcing student presents no such cognition for anything to support — "offloading" smuggles in the flattering assumption that thinking occurred somewhere and merely changed venue. Nothing was offloaded because nothing was ever loaded. The refinement: in Hutchins' terms, cognition about the task *did* occur — in the distributed system. What collapsed is the human's role within it, from functional component to signature-bearing relay. I'd suggest that's the more defensible formulation, because it preempts the obvious objection ("but the paper got written, so cognition happened") while sharpening the pedagogical loss: learning requires the cognition to run *through* the student, and in outsourcing the system routes around them entirely. The subtraction test then marks the boundary operationally, and Grice supplies the communicative description: offloading keeps the human in the conversation; outsourcing removes them from it while leaving their name on the utterance.

Last thing, and it's a gift for the bibliography: if the program is Human-AI *Communication* with an iterative, cyclical unit of analysis, the missing thinker is Herbert Clark — no relation to Andy. *Using Language* (1996) builds communication as joint action from exactly the unit you're asking for: the *contribution*, which is only complete when a presentation phase is met by an acceptance phase — the hearer providing evidence of understanding sufficient for current purposes, with grounding accumulating as common ground across the exchange. That's the formal alternative to the prompt-response dyad: not message-sent, but contribution-completed. And it hands you instruments immediately. Uncritical acceptance becomes a *degenerate acceptance phase* — closure with zero evidence of understanding. Repair initiation, clarification requests, and challenges become countable behavioral markers of the hearer doing Gricean work — which maps directly onto Kember's reflection levels and gives you something to code CT transcripts for. The student who outsources never initiates repair; the conductor in a CT session initiates it constantly, because the divergence forces it. That's your architecture-versus-willpower claim, rendered measurable in a communication theorist's native units.

**Fable said:**

The rehabilitation works, but it runs into a wrinkle in the founding text that turns out to be the most interesting thing about your proposal. Clark and Chalmers didn't extend mind unconditionally — Otto's notebook counts as memory because it meets trust criteria, and one of those criteria is *automatic endorsement*: Otto doesn't interrogate what he reads there, he just believes it, the way Inga believes her biological recall. For a notebook, that's fine — it's a static store of Otto's own past cognition; endorsing it is endorsing himself. But apply the criteria to a generative system and the valence flips. The LLM is not a repository of what the student put there; it generates novel content from an alien prior. Automatic endorsement, the very condition that *qualifies* the notebook as extended mind, is precisely the uncritical acceptance that disqualifies the AI exchange as learning. So your framework isn't just borrowing Extended Mind — it's amending it for a class of resource Clark and Chalmers never anticipated: cognitive onboarding is the *replacement* for the automatic-endorsement criterion when the external resource is generative rather than storative. A notebook extends the mind through trust; a generator extends it only through critical uptake, because trust without uptake doesn't extend the agent — it dissolves them into a relay. That's a publishable philosophical claim on its own, and it gives onboarding a principled definition rather than just a name for the return arrow.

And onboarding has more lineage waiting for it than you may realize — starting inside your own corpus. What you're describing is Vygotskian internalization applied to the human-AI dyad: higher functions appearing first *between* participants and only subsequently *within* the learner. Wertsch's refinement of Vygotsky gives you the quality gradient — his distinction between *mastery* of a cultural tool (skilled use) and *appropriation* (making it one's own) — and the outsourcing student achieves neither, which is further support for your claim that nothing was offloaded. But the sharpest instrument is one you already built: the Porosity Tetra's Witness, whose assigned function is discriminating "real accommodation (schema restructuring) versus new material being reprocessed in existing terms." That is exactly onboarding's quality metric — Piagetian accommodation versus disguised assimilation — designed for a different Tetra before you had the term. Porosity, seen from this angle, is an onboarding-quality monitor you built without naming what it monitors.

Now the part I most want to hand you, because your closing line about "generative in truth" is not just rhetoric — the pun is already sitting in the education literature, loaded and waiting. Wittrock's *generative learning theory* (1974 onward) holds that comprehension occurs only when the learner actively generates relations between new material and prior knowledge — generation is the learner's act, not the source's. Fiorella and Mayer's *Learning as a Generative Activity* (2015) operationalized it into concrete strategies: summarizing in one's own words, self-explaining, teaching back, mapping, enacting. Which means your reclamation of "generative" isn't a rhetorical inversion, it's a technically precise one: in "Generative AI" the generation happens in the model, and Wittrock's entire point is that model-side generation produces zero learning until matched by learner-side generation. Cognitive onboarding *is* Wittrock generation applied to AI output — and Fiorella and Mayer hand you the measurable behavioral battery for it. A student who self-explains the AI's response, teaches it back, or restates it against their prior knowledge is onboarding; one who transcribes is not. Stack that with Herbert Clark's acceptance phase and Kember's reflection levels from our last exchange and you now have three convergent coding schemes for the same construct, from communication theory, education psychology, and reflection research respectively — triangulated instrumentation for the thing your architecture claims to produce.

Two structural observations to close the loop. First, the two edges are coupled, and that coupling is where "iterative" earns its meaning: offloading quality constrains onboarding possibility. The Quantity-starved prompt from our Grice discussion yields output the student lacks the context to evaluate — you can only critically receive what you critically requested. But each genuine onboarding enriches the next offload, because the common ground has grown; the loop compounds. That compounding is the difference between a cycle and a spiral, and it's what makes the process generative rather than merely repetitive. Second, CT's no-persistence architecture turns out to enforce your thesis mechanically: when the session evaporates on window close, the distributed system's common ground is destroyed, and whatever was onboarded into the student is the *only surviving residue*. That's the subtraction test run temporally — close the window and see what remains — and it means your privacy feature and your learning theory are the same design decision viewed from two sides.

Underneath all of it, I'd name the pattern, because your corpus keeps making one claim in different vocabularies: heteroglossia lives in the instantiation between; meaning fruition comes only in the response between; mind develops between people before within them; transitional space sits between inner and outer; the tetrahedron's value is in its edges, not its vertices. Yours is a relational ontology of human-AI interaction, and offloading and onboarding are simply its two directed edges — the between, given arrows. That's the sentence I'd want somewhere near the front of the fellowship proposal.
